  • Session 5 - Plato
    Oligarchy has a way of justifying itself through ideas (examples of this are Mussolini's doctrine of fascism , James Henry Hammond's mudsill theory , Alexander Stephens' cornerstone speech , Walter Lippmann's notion of the bewildered herd, John Calhoun and James McGill Buchanan's public choice theory, and many others). The mistrust of democracy is old. The founders of the US were concerned about this, as was Plato almost 2500 years ago, after Socrates was sentenced to death by a democratic vote. His struggle with this concept, and his radical solution-which at its heart, separates power and wealth-- remains highly controversial (because to some it offers justifications for authoritarianism), yet in the process of developing his arguments, he touched upon many key concepts (the distinction between knowledge and belief, a tripartite division of the human psyche, a critique of art, and notions of censorship). It isn't clear if Plato meant for his formula to be taken literally, ironically (the dialog itself may not have passed the censorship rules it itself proposes), or simply as a way to force certain deep intellectual tensions out in the open.

    • Discussion aired on 18 Dec 1970 on the Dick Cavett show between Lester Maddox, the segregationist governor of Georgia, and Jim Brown.
      A little before minute 3, Maddox cites 'laws of God' and 'natural law' as he argues for racial segregation, against so-called civil laws in place that mandated integration (on the right at the time 'Brown vs Board of Education' was not in the distant past, and even more recently in the 1960s, significant civil rights legislation had been passed. If you get a chance to listen to 2-3 minutes of this video (from where I have time-stamped it in the link) prior to our session next week, that would be great. This demonstrates that even very recently (indeed even now) the concept of natural law survives and colors our discourses.
